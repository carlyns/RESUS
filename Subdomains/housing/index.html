Common Legal Structures

Building type and social structure are distinct from the legal structure governing your group.

Option A. Incorporating as a nonprofit organization (cooperative) is a popular structure because it is flexible and the most democratic. The organization owns the property, and each member has an equal right to occupy a unit and a vote in decision-making. This structure has been used for market-rate high-rise units to scattered-site single family homes. It is commonly coupled with the limited equity model.

Option B. Your group might also decide to simply get on the title together, but how your group manages itself and makes decisions is less formalized. This is known as tenants-in-common.

Limited Equity

An existing framework that guarantees long-term investment in the community.

Limiting equity means capping the value of property so it remains affordable for future generations. The cap is decided by the residents.

It allows residents to make a fair return on investment if they ever need to move on, but doesn’t allow developers to profit absurdly if the neighborhood happens to gentrify.
